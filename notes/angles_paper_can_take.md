# Angle: reaction to scores and ratios of staff

## Patient (demand) Side:
Satisfaction scores and ratios of MD/PA/NP impact patients. Since patients are insured (and I'm looking at Medicare) the price is fixed -- what's not fixed is the number of services, which is chosen by the provider. That's why patients (might) react to satisfaction scores and probability of seeing an MD vs. non-MD (if the patient cares about that -- this is idiosyncratic, so taste for provider type varies by patient, and since many people initially see non-MDs first and then the MD steps in if the patient warrants higher acuity, then this is much more out of their hands. Definitely out of their hands in the inpatient setting)

Would be great to try to estimate patient preferences for NP/PAs vs. MDs. 


## Provider (supply) Side: 
Provider chooses the number of services to provide. They care about patient health, costs, outcomes, etc. but some providers are more altruistic than others. Providers may or may not care about patient satisfaction (channel operates either through taste for money or altruism, as no taste for money and/or, since they are linked, high moral altruism may mean the providers care about delivering the best care, not the one that will just raise satisfaction scores)

For theory: providers are the ones who choose the quantity of service. This is controlled by their level of altruism -- some providers do more services for the sake of the patient (think of advanced providers who handle high-acuity cases rather than sending them off via referral to someon else) or because they want to bill more (i.e. motivated by money). Some are lazy and don't want to see patients, while others don't want to increase the costs the patient faces (although this is muted for Medicare patients). All of this is idiosyncratic. 

## Hospital (organization/firm) Side:
Hospital systems are price takers in the market for providers. However, they choose the quantity of MDs, PAs, and NPs to higher -- which indirectly impacts the number of services delivered to the patients, which then impacts quality (via outcomes), perception (via scores and costs to the patients), and, thus, patient choice. Hospitals absolutely care about patient satisfaction scores, and this enters into their decision making equation. 

For theory: hospitals choose the number of providers, and more specifically number of MDs to PAs to NPs. As already said, they are price takers (and labor market for providers assumed to be frictionless; not reality but not a stretch, given the shortage of medical personnel), so that's why instead they choose quantity. This indirectly impacts the quantity of services, since providers choose the quantity of services, but first-order is hospital chooses ratios of MD/PA/NP.
